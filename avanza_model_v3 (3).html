Investor return table
If each capital call is a separate investor — returns by vintage

Because the book is funded with a fresh capital call each year, you can place each call with a different investor. This shows what each one earns if all exit together at the end of the horizon, with annual distributions split pro-rata to capital outstanding. Key result: IRR is roughly flat across all vintages — splitting the raise across many investors does not raise the rate of return, because IRR is a per-dollar-per-year rate. What changes is MOIC, which rises with hold period (an early investor compounds the pref over more years). The real lever multiple investors unlock is re-pricing later tranches cheaper as the book de-risks — that lowers blended cost of funds and lifts the operator residual, it does not change any single investor's IRR.

Management economics (carry above hurdle)
Traditional LP/GP (Investor / Management) Structure
Standard private credit fund structure:
Investor (senior tranche) receives 100% of their preferred return / coupon — typically 8–12% for credit funds, 6–8% for lower-risk vehicle lending.
Above the hurdle, profits split 80% operator / 20% investor kicker — this is the universal baseline. Senior managers in top-performing credit funds negotiate 70/30. First-time funds typically accept 80/20 or even 85/15.
For Avanza specifically: given the Venezuela risk premium, 80/20 is the right anchor. If you can demonstrate consistent returns above hurdle in Year 1–2, renegotiate to 75/25 at next capital raise.
Comparable: Credifamilia (COL) GP carries 20%. Credi-Q structured products use 80/20. LatAm specialty lenders generally 80/20 with 10–12% hurdle.

IRR — annualized return on the investor's cash flows: initial capital out (Year 0), annual distributions in (Years 1–N), plus return of principal at end of horizon. Calculated via Newton-Raphson.  |  MOIC — total cash returned ÷ capital invested, measured over the selected horizon. Always specify the time period alongside MOIC — a 2× over 3 years is very different from 2× over 8 years.  |  DPI — distributions paid in only (excludes terminal principal return). Shows how much cash the investor has received before exit.  |  Capital payback — year when cumulative distributions alone (before terminal return) recover the initial investment.  |  Carry — management's share of profits above the hurdle rate, earned only after investor preferred return is fully satisfied each year.  |  Important: the investor's cost is already embedded in the blended cost of capital charged to the loan portfolio in the P&L. The investor tab shows how that cost is distributed as cash flows — it does not add an additional charge on top of the P&L.

● Funding & Capital Plan
Capital required

The book must be funded by cash. Investor capital is a revolving senior commitment sized to inv% × outstanding book at all times — it scales as the book grows and is only returned to the investor at exit / run-off. Operator funds the first-loss remainder. A capital call is the new cash needed when the book grows; a return (in parentheses) is capital handed back when the book shrinks. Calls are sized to the ending (peak) book each period — the cash you must actually hold — while cost-of-funds in the P&L is charged on the average deployed balance.

Annual capital plan
Quarterly capital schedule (20 quarters)

Capital-call timing assumes a linear ramp through Year 1 (book builds from zero) and geometric growth thereafter, distributed evenly across quarters. Returns of capital appear when the book contracts (e.g. bear / run-off). This schedule is what an investor term sheet and a treasury cash-flow plan are built from: total Year-1 cash to launch, the pace of follow-on calls, and the peak balance the structure must hold.
